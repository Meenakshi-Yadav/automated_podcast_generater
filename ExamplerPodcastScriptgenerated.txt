About 120 years ago, the indecisive, dilettante offspring of a wealthy physician found these questions so important that he took up his pen to argue that, no, books are never the instruments of moral betterment. He based his argument on his own memories. Though he had always been an avid reader, books, he claimed, never gave him any sort of useful, respectable instruction.

Hindsight makes it easy to find in Marcel Proust’s essay On Reading (1905) the spark that would later flare into his multivolume novel, In Search of Lost Time (1913-27). However, his essay was not the intuition of solitary genius. Rather, it was published as a preface to the French translation of John Ruskin’s Sesame and Lilies (1865) – a cryptic title bringing together two lectures Ruskin delivered in Manchester in December 1864.

*Host (as a note to listeners): This gives us context about where Proust's essay was published and its connection to John Ruskin's work.*

[Chime]
Host: So, the word "[cryptic]" here means mysterious or hard to understand.

In his lectures, Ruskin bemoaned the general spiritual impoverishment of Victorian Britain, where every purpose has been ‘infected’ with ‘the idea that everything should “pay”’. The problem with this mindset, says Ruskin, is that it makes books superfluous – because genuine literacy is a training in disinterestedness, in generously reflecting on the meaning of chosen expressions.

*Host (as a note to listeners): This explains Ruskin's criticism of the Victorian mindset and its impact on reading.*

[Chime]
Host: So, the word "[disinterestedness]" here means impartiality or lack of selfish interest.

Provocatively, Ruskin describes his generation as illiterate – even in a time when education was expanding. He thought his contemporaries had lost any capacity for understanding each other or any important issue because they read superficially, and for the wrong reasons – chiefly, to get social recognition from a narrow group of peers.

*Host (as a note to listeners): This highlights Ruskin's concern about shallow reading and its impact on society.*

QUESTION 1 (Main Idea):
What is Proust's primary argument about reading, according to the essay?
A) Reading is a form of moral education.
B) Books are never the instruments of moral betterment.
C) Mediocre books can contribute to self-discovery.
D) Genuine literacy is a training in disinterestedness.

Host: The correct answer is B) Books are never the instruments of moral betterment. Let's discuss why. Option A is incorrect because Proust disagreed with Ruskin's belief that reading should be considered a means to moral education. Option C is not directly related to the main argument but rather supports Proust's view. Option D is one of Ruskin's points about literacy, but it does not represent Proust's primary argument.

QUESTION 2 (Inference):
What can we infer about Ruskin's views on shallow reading in Victorian Britain?
A) He approved of it.
B) He found it beneficial for social recognition.
C) He believed it was widespread and harmful to society.
D) He thought it improved understanding among people.

Host: The correct answer is C) He believed it was widespread and harmful to society. [Explanation]: Ruskin criticized his contemporaries for reading superficially, focusing on social recognition rather than genuine understanding. This suggests that he found shallow reading to be a problem in Victorian Britain.

QUESTION 3 (Detail/Function):
Why did Proust write the essay "On Reading" as a preface to John Ruskin’s Sesame and Lilies?
A) To introduce Ruskin's work to French readers.
B) To express his disagreement with Ruskin's views on reading.
C) To share his personal memories related to reading.
D) To promote the idea of moral education through reading.

Host: The correct answer is B) To express his disagreement with Ruskin's views on reading. [Explanation]: Proust wrote the essay as a preface to Ruskin’s work because he had strong opinions about Ruskin's defense of reading and felt the need to express his disagreement.
